2 AC Circuits

Alternating current (AC) circuits involve potential differences and currents which vary periodically — often sinusoidally — with time. The time-dependent response of an AC circuit to a sinusoidal driving signal consists of a transient component that dies off due to resistive losses, and a sinusoidal steady-state component. We will focus on the steady-state behavior of the AC circuits we consider.

2.1 Resistance, Reactance, and Impedance: the Complex Number Representation

When driven by a sinusoidal signal, the resulting currents through and potential differences across capacitors, inductors, and resistors are all sinusoidal as well, but with different, constant, phase relationships to each other.


CIVIL
The mnemonic CIVIL can be used to remember that the voltage across a capacitor lags (half a period of oscillation) behind the current (CIV), and the voltage across an inductor leads the current by (VIL). The voltage across a resistor is always in phase with the current.

Any network of linear devices — inductors, resistors, and capacitors — present a complex impedance to the flow of current. The total impedance can be found by combining the real resistances and imaginary reactances in the circuit in the same way that we determined equivalent resistance in Section 1.2. The inductive and capacitive reactances and hence the total impedance are frequency-dependent. In the low-frequency limit, capacitors dominate the impedance, while in the high-frequency limit, inductors dominate.
